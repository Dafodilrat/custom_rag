Abstract
This research explores the use of Vision-Language Mod-
els (VLMs) for robot manipulation using natural language.
The project is divided into two phases, with Phase 1 evalu-
ating basic motion control using a Large Language Model
(LLM) alone, while Phase 2 integrated visual perception
through a Visual Language Model (VLM) to help Sawyer
with object manipulation tasks. The system demonstrated
strong ability in allowing natural language to motion trans-
lation in simulation, paving the way for real-world deploy-
ment.
1. Introduction
Modern automation systems have generally relied
on well-defined structured control protocols, by the use
of state machines or pre-defined behaviors. While the
techniques have specific purposes that work well, they limit
the adaptability of robots within complex and dynamic
environments. In contrast, natural language presents a
simple process that allows people to interact without the
need for robot programming or motion planning.
Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) [9]
and Vision-Language Models (VLMs) [7] have enabled the
ability to map complex human commands to basic robot
actions. For example, large language models as GPT-4
can understand and parse free-form text, while VLMs
further enable grounding of language in visual contexts.
In robotics, for example, these models allow users to
command robots in the everyday language, specifying
objects, spatial relations, and goals flexibly.
However, integrating LLMs and VLMs into real-world
robotic control presents multiple challenges. These in-
clude the need for accurate perception, transformation
of language-based goals into executable robotic motion
instructions, also ensuring safety in dynamic environments.
In this project, we explore these challenges by developing
a modular framework wherein Sawyer robot, a 7-DOF
collaborative robotic arm [9], can interpret natural language
instructions, perceive its environment visually, and plan
safe trajectories to perform activities independently.
The major goal is to describe a complete pipeline
that involves processing natural language input, through
perception and reasoning, to motion planning and execu-
tion, setting the core phase for language-driven robotic
manipulation in real-world applications.
The project was split into two separate phases:
1. Phase 1: Test text-to-motion capability using an LLM,
interpreting commands like ”move to (0.5, 0.2).”
2. Phase 2: Integrate vision by utilizing VLMs to locate
objects and execute pick-and-place tasks in response
to human instructions.
2. Literature Review
Recent advancements in vision-language models(VLM)
have significantly enhanced the ability of robots to in-
terpret natural language instructions in complex visual
environments. However, most existing VLMs struggle with
understanding spatial relationships such as ”left”, ”behind”,
”closest to the blue object”, which are critical for grounded
robotic manipulation.To address this, SpatialVLM by Chen
[2] enhances vision-language models with explicit spatial
reasoning by training on a large-scale 3D spatial VQA
dataset. It enables accurate interpretation of metric spatial
relationships from 2D images, addressing a core limitation
in conventional VLMs. This makes it a strong foundation
for robotic systems that rely on spatially grounded language
understanding for manipulation tasks.
Task-VLM [1] introduces a framework for task-oriented
robotic manipulation by incorporating spatial relationships
and object-level attributes like mass, fragility, and material.
The model constructs spatial relation trees from visual and
textual inputs, allowing structured reasoning over complex
tasks.This enables more interpretable and physically in-
formed manipulation decisions, complementing purely spa-
tial models like SpatialVLM .RoboPoint [14] further ad-
vances spatial grounding by predicting manipulation key-
points directly from language instructions. By learning
to map commands to actionable 3D points, it eliminates
reliance on symbolic reasoning and improves precision
in real-world pick-and-place tasks.Physically Grounded
VLMs[3] expand this reasoning by integrating physical ob-
ject attributes such as weight, fragility, and material. This
allows the model to infer safe and context-aware manipu-
lation strategies, which are critical when interacting with
objects that require delicate handling.
RoboFlamingo [6] builds on the OpenFlamingo architecture
to adapt pre-trained VLMs for robotic manipulation through
imitation learning. By fine-tuning on robot-specific data
with minimal supervision it enables generalization to un-
seen manipulation tasks while maintaining efficiency. This
approach demonstrates how lightweight adaptation of large
VLMs can support real-world policy learning without end-
to-end retraining.RobotGPT [4] introduces a framework in
which a large language model, such as ChatGPT, is used
to generate high-level task plans and code-based guidance.
The generated output is transferred to a task specific net-
work,which is trained to execute tasks reliably in real-time
without relying on the original language model. This two-
stage setup improves task consistency and reliability, partic-
ularly in long-horizon manipulation scenarios, while avoid-
ing dependence on large-scale models during deployment.
ChatGPT-Empowered Long-Step Robot Control [12] intro-
duces a framework for leveraging large language models
in multi-stage robotic planning through structured prompt
templates. The method decomposes high-level language in-
structions into temporally ordered sub-tasks using few-shot
in-context learning, enabling robust policy guidance across
extended task sequences. By grounding prompts in visual
observations and spatial context, the approach facilitates
reasoning over long-horizon manipulation tasks without re-
quiring task-specific fine-tuning. Vision-Language Model-
Driven scene understanding and Robotics Object Manipu-
lation [8] presents an integrated pipeline that combines vi-
sual perception, natural language understanding, and code
generation for robotic manipulation in unstructured envi-
ronments. The system employs a pre-trained VLM based on
LLaMA2-chat to generate a semantic description of scenes,
followed by zero-shot object grounding and pose estima-
tion. These outputs are then passed to a code-generating
language model that synthesizes high-level control com-
mands, bridging the gap between perception, instruction,
and execution in tabletop manipulation tasks.
CLIPort [10] introduces a two-stream architecture that
combines CLIP-based semantic understanding with spa-
tial precision from Transporter Networks.It performs zero-
shot language-conditioned manipulation by aligning text
prompts with dense pixel-level action maps, demonstrat-
ing strong performance in real-world pick-and-place tasks
without requiring explicit object detection or symbolic
planning.GPT-4V(ision) for Robotics [13] extends this di-
rection by enabling robots to learn from one-shot human
demonstrations using multimodal reasoning. The system
combines visual analysis, symbolic task planning, and spa-
tiotemporal grounding to extract affordances and gener-
ate executable manipulation strategies from demonstration
videos, highlighting the potential of general-purpose VLMs
in robot imitation learning.
3. Environment
3.1. Robot Operating System (ROS)
The Robot Operating System (ROS) is a flexible frame-
work for developing robotic applications. It provides a
plethora of tools, libraries, and conventions that aim to
simplify the creation of complex and robust robot behav-
iors. ROS facilitates communication between different
components of a robot system, such as sensors, actuators,
controllers, and higher-level algorithms, through a dis-
tributed computing architecture. It offers various features,
including message passing, service calls, parameter man-
agement, visualization tools, and simulation capabilities.
ROS is widely used in both research and industry across
a diverse range of robotic applications, including but not
limited to autonomous navigation, manipulation, percep-
tion, mapping, localization, and human-robot interaction.
It has a large and active community that contributes to its
development and maintenance, providing extensive docu-
mentation, tutorials, and software packages. ROS supports
multiple programming languages, primarily C++, Python,
and recently, Rust. Overall, ROS greatly accelerates the de-
velopment and deployment of robotic systems by provid-
ing a standardized and modular framework that fosters col-
laboration and innovation in the robotics community. Its
versatility and scalability make it an indispensable tool for
researchers, developers, and engineers working on robotic
projects of all scales and complexities.
3.2. Unified Robot Description Format(URDF)
URDF, or Unified Robot Description Format, is an
XML-based file format used to describe robotic systems in
a standardized and structured manner. It provides a way to
represent the physical properties and geometry of robots, in-
cluding links, joints, sensors, and visual and collision prop-
erties. URDF files are commonly used in conjunction with
the Robot Operating System (ROS) to describe robots for
simulation, visualization, and control. URDFfiles define the
kinematic and dynamic properties of robots, allowing for
accurate modeling and simulation of robot behavior. They
enable visualization of robots in 3D environments and col-
lision checking for path planning and manipulation tasks.
URDF files can also be used to specify robot configurations
and launch files for ROS-based applications.
3.3. Gazebo Open Source Simulator
Gazebo serves as a comprehensive 3D simulator, capa-
ble of simulating both indoor and outdoor environments
with multiple robots. It includes dynamic and kinematic
physics simulations and supports various physics engines
that can be easily plugged in. The integration of Gazebo
with the Robot Operating System (ROS) is facilitated by a
set of Gazebo plugins designed to support a wide range of
existing robots and sensors. These plugins share a message
interface consistent with the rest of the ROS ecosystem.
This compatibility allows developers to write ROS nodes
that work seamlessly with simulation, recorded data, and
physical hardware. This means that you can create and test
applications in the simulation environment and then deploy
them to a real robot with minimal or no code changes.

4. Methodology
4.1. System Architecture
The architectural model in Figure 2 describes the com-
plete process for implementing robotic applications using
VLM for the Sawyer robotic arm. The process begins with
a photo taken by the camera in the robot’s head-mounted
vision sensor. This image, along with a natural language in-
struction, is passed to the Vision-Language Model (VLM),
which interprets the scene and the task. The VLM predicts
a 2D pixel location (u, v) corresponding to the target ob-
ject within the image. This 2D pixel is then combined with
depth information to back-project the point into a 3D po-
sition in the camera frame. Subsequently, the 3D point is
converted into the robot’s underlying coordinate system to
provide a target location (x, y, z) in the real world. An in-
verse kinematics solver calculates a suitable joint configu-
ration that would enable the end-effector to this target pose.
MoveIt is used for trajectory planning to ensure collision-
free and dynamically feasible motion. Finally, the generated
motion plan is physically executed on the robot, produc-
ing the goal-directed manipulation task. This component-
based architecture ensures that perception, reasoning, plan-
ning, and execution are cleanly decoupled while maintain-
ing overall system robustness.

4.2. LLM-Based Command Parsing
In the early stages of its development, a Large Language
Model was utilized to parse natural language commands
into structured robot instructions. When the LLM receives
a user request, a preexisting JSON structure that includes
the given actions, such as repositioning, closing, or en-
gaging the gripping mechanism and lifting the object, are
coupled actions linked to a specific API function call in the
robotic control framework. The template for the prompt
was carefully designed to ensure that the LLM consistently
produces well-structured and executable outputs, even
for varying natural language formulations. This method
abstracts low-level control complexities from the user,
enabling intuitive interaction and ensuring that the robot
can execute the instructions without needing additional
semantic parsing. Validation of command parsing through
the use of LLMs was first performed, hence creating a
strong foundation for extending the system to incorporate
vision in the next phase.

4.3. Vision-Language Model Integration
In the next stage, the system was improved to incor-
porate visual perception by adding a Vision-Language
framework. The Vision-Language Model (VLM) takes
both a camera image along with a natural language directive
as its input and predicts a 2D pixel location (u, v) of the
target object in the image. Simultaneously, it outputs a
structured system of activities necessary for the accomplish
the objective, similar to the LLM-based model. This
approach enables grounding of natural language commands
in visual observations, allowing the robot to independently
detect objects and determine appropriate motion goals. A
highly structured prompt instructs the VLM to produce
consistent outputs, thus ensuring a uniform engagement
with downstream processes coordinate transformation and
action execution modules. The integration of visual input
and linguistic processing enables the robot to interact more
intelligently within unstructured spaces without being
weighed down by prespecified object identifiers or manual
target selection.

5. VLM Model Performance Comparison
Several Vision-Language Models (VLMs) were eval-
uated to determine their suitability for robotic system
control. The tasks are rooted in two core measures:
inference speed and localization accuracy. The models
tested in The models released included GPT-4 Turbo
[9], LLaV A 1.5 [7], Gemini 2.0 Flash, and Claude 3
Opus. Through the use of an image along with the
corresponding verbal instructions, the object’s expected
coordinates were compared to the corresponding ground
truth labels to evaluate the Euclidean localization error.
In addition, average inference time per query was measured.
As indicated in Figure 6, the GPT-4 Turbo exhibited
better localization because there was a lower Euclidean
distance and has an error margin of 8.06 pixels along with
a moderate inference latency of 3.22 seconds. LLaV A
1.5 provided faster reaction times (2.19 seconds), though
reduced accuracy (27.73 pixels error). Gemini 2.0 Flash
and The Claude 3 Opus showed a higher inference rate but
suffered from high localization inaccuracies making them
unsuitable for precise manipulation tasks.
Since accurate localization of targets is crucial for
successful robotic grasping, accuracy was given more
priority over inference speed. GPT-4 Turbo was therefore
selected as the base VLM architecture for this system,
ensuring robust task execution even at the cost of slightly
slower response times.

5.1. Inverse Kinematics and Motion Execution
The system takes an RGB image from the robot’s
head-mounted camera, which is then processed using a
Vision-Language Model (VLM) to predict the coordinates
of the center pixel (u, v), which is linked to target object.
Simultaneously, a depth image from the camera provides
depth information for each pixel, allowing the system
to retrieve the depth d at the predicted (u, v) location.
The 2D pixel location and the depth information is then
incorporated using intrinsic camera calibration parameters
to enable back-projection of the point into a 3D position in
the camera coordinate frame.
To plan robot motion, the 3D point in the camera
frame must be transformed into the robot’s base frame.
This is achieved through TF2 pose transformations, thus
ensuring all specified poses are properly expressed relative
to the robot’s fixed coordinate system. The resulting 3D
target pose (x, y, z) defines the desired location of the
end-effector in the real -world environment.
An inverse kinematics solver, using MoveIt, selects a
suitable joint configuration which achieves the target pose.
In case a direct inverse kinematics solution is not possible,
the system considers proximate points to set a realistic and
achievable goal that builds resilience. Having determined
an appropriate configuration of joints, MoveIt is further
used to generate a complete collision-free trajectory,
respecting joint limits, velocityand acceleration constraints,
and obstacle avoidance.
At the higher level of task planning, the user instructions
are converted by the VLM to structured form using a set
of designated application programming interface functions,
such as move to(), close gripper(), and open gripper(), lift().
Those operations are interpreted and executed in real time
dynamically, thus enabling the Sawyer robot to respond in-
dependently autonomously react to human language inputs.

6. Results
Both components of the project were successfully veri-
fied, i.e., LLM-based control and VLM-based control.
In Phase 1, the LLM-based system was deployed on the
real Sawyer robot, where it reliably parsed natural language
instructions like the one ”move to (0.5, 0.2)” were con-
verted to their corresponding actions using inverse kinemat-
ics and trajectory planning. The robot consistently reached
the specified target poses without collisions. This well illus-
trates the viability of the language-to-motion model when
applied to physical hardware.
In Phase 2, the VLM-based system was thoroughly tested
in the Gazebo simulation environment. The system prop-
erly understood complex linguistic commands like ”pick up
the blue cube”, through the implementation of the Vision-
Language Model to detect objects and then design related
motion trajectories. The robot showed the ability to inde-
pendently identify target objects, define possible movement
strategies, and execute pick-and-place actions, within the
simulated environment, thus demonstrating the practicality
of VLM-based robotics manipulation.
7. Conclusion and Future Work
This research demonstrated the feasibility of combining
vision-language models with large language models to
achieve natural language-driven robotic control. Through
a modular pipeline combining perception, reasoning, plan-
ning, and execution, the Sawyer robot was able to interpret
high-level natural language instructions and autonomously
perform motion planning towards designated destinations.
Future research will focus on implementing the system
in its entirety on real-world hardware, along with improv-
ing the framework to deal with increasingly complex, multi-
step manipulation tasks. Improvements in refining vision-
language models for robotics-specific datasets [15], [11],
[5] can notably enhance the reliability of perception. Fur-
thermore, dynamic environment handling and real-time re-
planning are important directions to expand the system’s
significance.
